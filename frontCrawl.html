Front Crawl

Crucible is a relatively easy style to learn, comfortable to perform and economical in terms of resistance. In the crawl, swimmers use an alternate arm action and foot shake. A full cycle of this style is comprised of a full right arm action, a full left arm action, and a variable number of leg shakes. Leg movement is also called "swing kick" and consists of an alternate leg movement starting at the hip, in an up and down motion, with the legs relaxed, the feet inward and the toes pointed. Different rhythms are used in leg action with respect to coordination between arms and legs, depending on the swimmer and the distance to be performed. The prevailing foot shakes are 6, 4 and 2 shakes. Thus, long distance swimmers usually perform 4 or 2 shakes and speed 6 swimmers. In the crawl style, air is taken, inhaled through the mouth, turning the head to the side, and the air is expelled. exhale, underwater.


techniques

Be as horizontal as possible:

the head should be aligned with the rest of the body. Find a horizontal posture with minimal resistance to advance, forgetting to stand out from the water. Look towards down (not forward) so your legs and hips don't They will sink and you will not get so tired.

Control breathing:

release the air into the water and take it when the mouth comes out of the water, coinciding with the recovery phase of an arm. Try to breathe on both sides, alternately. The head is the only thing that turns.

Move arms and legs:

the lower extremities help us move forward and maintain balance. Move your entire leg from the hip, not just the ankles. The arms move in two phases: the aquatic and the aerial .

Coordinate movements:

the crawl style works like a well-oiled machine, everything has to fit in the right moment. The synchronization of the legs, arms and head will help you to propel yourself better and to tire less.

Counting strokes:

swim with the minimum number of strokes to avoid energy wear.

Swimming in a straight line:

to avoid colliding, take the pool lines.

Lengthen your stroke:

put your elbow up while pulling your arm back into the water. This position, which allows the forearm to be held vertically for a longer time, increases grip in the water and improves propulsion.

Turn the body:

do not swim flat, but roll from one side to the other. In this way, you activate the back and shoulder muscles and give you more strength in the stroke.
